# JTBD Analysis — landing-rework

**Phase:** DIVERGE / Phase 1
**Feature ID:** landing-rework
**Date:** 2026-04-29

---

## 1. Raw Request

Rethink the Systemix landing page. Current landing leads with "Evidence Layer for design systems" and the H1 "Every component is a guess until production proves it." Designer, developer, and business-partner feedback reveals the message is unclear: designers don't understand the setup or the Figma story; developers don't understand the integration path (MCP? CLI? Hermes? agents?); partners don't know what kind of infrastructure it requires.

Founder's repositioning signal: "The real moat is the learning loop — not design drift." The hypothesis validation is the main value prop. The landing should communicate what Systemix actually is as a platform — agents that write .md files, a learning loop, a system that closes the feedback cycle from production back to the contract.

---

## 2. Job Extraction — 5 Why Chain

**Starting point (as stated):** "We need to rethink the landing page."

**Why do we need to rethink the landing page?**
Because visitors aren't understanding what Systemix does or why they would use it.

**Why aren't they understanding it?**
Because the current framing leads with "evidence layer" and "drift" — which are outcome labels, not experiential explanations. They don't know what job Systemix does for them in the first five seconds.

**Why does the framing fail even though the underlying product is differentiated?**
Because the real differentiation — that every design decision is a hypothesis that gets measured and written back into the contract — is buried in the HowItWorks section, not the hero. The hero talks about what the system produces (evidence), not what it enables the team to do (continuously validate and learn from design decisions).

**Why does it matter that the enabling action is in the hero, not the output?**
Because the buyer (AI-native design engineer, Series A–C startup) has already tried syncing tools (Knapsack, Fragments, Figma) and been disappointed. "Evidence layer" sounds like another sync tool. The new promise needs to be: this is how your team keeps making better decisions, not just better documentation.

**Why does "making better decisions" resonate more than "better documentation"?**
Because design engineers at AI-native startups are drowning in generated output (agents shipping tokens, LLMs producing variants) and their actual pain is: they can't tell which generated output is correct. The job is to maintain calibration — to know which design decisions have been validated so agents can act on them rather than re-litigating them.

**5 Whys result:** The job is not "rethink the landing" and not "explain what the product does." The job is: give the team a way to know which design decisions are grounded in evidence and which are guesses — so that when agents ship, humans approve, or experiments run, everyone starts from known ground.

---

## 3. Job Statements

### Functional Job (primary)

When my AI-assisted team is shipping design decisions faster than they can be validated, I want to know which decisions are grounded in production evidence and which are still hypotheses, so I can give my agents and my team a reliable baseline to build from instead of re-litigating the same decisions every sprint.

### Emotional Job

When I see my agent ship a token variant I already tested and rejected three months ago, I want to feel that the system has memory — that the learning my team accumulated is preserved and accessible, not scattered across Slack threads and stale Notion pages, so I don't feel like every sprint starts from zero.

### Social Job

When I present a design decision to stakeholders or write a HITL review, I want to be able to show that the decision was validated by production data — not just asserted by a designer or generated by an LLM — so I am perceived as someone who ships evidence-informed design, not gut-feel design.

## 5. Disruption Check

**Is there a higher-level job that would make this entire job unnecessary?**

Yes: if LLMs could reliably generate correct design decisions from first principles without any production feedback, teams wouldn't need to validate them. But LLMs generate statistically probable output, not contextually validated output. The disruption check confirms the job is durable: as long as AI generates design decisions at scale, the need to validate and calibrate those decisions against production reality is structurally necessary.

**The job survives the disruption check.**

## 7. Opportunity Candidates — Most Under-Served

**ODI-2 (Re-proposing known-bad variants):** Extremely high importance to AI-native teams running agents at scale. No competitor explicitly addresses this. Satisfaction near zero — nothing on the market prevents an agent from generating a variant that was tried and rejected. Estimated score: 9 importance × 2 satisfaction = opportunity 16.

**ODI-5 (Shipping without evidence context):** High importance to design engineers who have been burned by agent-generated output that looked plausible but was context-free. Competitors offer documentation, not evidence. Estimated score: 8 importance × 2 satisfaction = opportunity 14.

**ODI-3 (Connecting experiment result to design artifact):** High importance to teams already running PostHog but not closing the loop. Nobody does this. Estimated score: 9 importance × 1 satisfaction = opportunity 17.

**ODI-6 (Integration ease):** High importance to developers who see Hermes/Ollama/MCP/agents/.md as a wall of setup. Estimated score: 8 importance × 3 satisfaction = opportunity 13. Addressed more by developer experience than by landing copy alone, but still a landing-page concern.
